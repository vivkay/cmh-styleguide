Principles

Our digital products aim to showcase the extraordinary events, objects, people and stories presented by the Canadian Museum of History and Canadian War Museum.

Goals

This document aims to present unified guidelines that ensure a high standard of consistency, quality and accessibility for our digital products.

Principles

Our digital products should be easy to use and fun to explore.

Easy to Use

Clean design and strategic placement of content can greatly enhance the user experience. As general guidelines, consider using:

  • A clear visual hierarchy of information.
  • Generous use of white space.
  • Relevant and concise text.
  • Easily identifiable navigational links.
  • Content filters or other tools to enable individual customization of content.

Fun to Explore

Our digital products will provide an engaging experience and tell compelling stories through:

  • Gorgeous photography or striking imagery.
  • Dynamic elements that respond to user interaction.
  • Fresh content (e.g. that integrates live social media updates).

Typography

The museum brand guidelines uses flat design–the use of solid colour fills and clean lines– which is to be consistent across all digital products. Specific treatments, such as gradients and drop-shadows, are not consistent with our branding guidelines. For more details, refer to our general Graphic Standards Styleguide.

Colour

The digital colour palettes were drawn from the existing branding guidelines, with some minor changes:

  • Additional shades of grey have been added to create a more visually robust and accessible digital aesthetic.
  • The brand grey, as well as the accent colours Red and Green have been modified for AA standard accessibility, using the free Colour Contrast Analyser as a point of reference.

Usage: Accent Colours

The vibrant accent colours (red for CMH and green for CWM) are reserved for high-priority content such as call-to-action or revenue-generating buttons. The placement of these accent colours throughout the page should be used to strategically direct the user’s eye across the page.

Contrast

A strong visual difference, or contrast, of colours is integral to accessibility. Keep in mind that reds and greens appear similar to grey tones for colour blind users. For this reason, the brand accent colours should never appear on a grey background.

Separated Text

If you are providing images, please separate all text. This is critical for web accessibility, optimal legibility, and visual consistency. Images with embedded text will not be applied to digital products.

Typography

Thoughtful typography is critical to creating an accessible and enjoyable user-experience. Key components of typography–hierarchy, contrast, legibility, leading (line-height), contrast, and alignment– are critical in the organization, presentation and prioritization of information.

Digital Fonts

The web fonts for the CMH/CWM brand are Futura (web version) and Arial (web version). These clean, crisp sans-serif fonts offer flexibility through various available web-sizes, and readability.

Intro or Deck

A deck–also known as an intro, stand-first or kicker–is the bridge between the heading and the body copy for a particular article. The deck is usually the introductory paragraph to an article, and can be styled differently to create visual hierarchy and interest.

  • Intro paragraphs are limited to 1 to 2 sentences in length.
  • Intro paragraph styling includes a larger point size, and optional variant of colour treatment.
  • If you are supplying text, please provide clearly identified intro text, separated from the body content.

Line Height

The line height, or leading of your text refers to the amount of spacing between each line. This should be calulated at about one-and-a-half times the size of your text. For example, if your text is set at 1 rem, your line height should be set at 1.5 rem.

Poor line height causes your text to collapse onto itself and reduces readability. This is not an ideal scenario, and will cause users to abandon your content.

Good line height allows your text room to breathe, and improves readability. For longer articles, it also facilitates your reader's ability to scan content, and find what they are looking for faster.
